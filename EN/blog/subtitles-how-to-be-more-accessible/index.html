Subtitles: How to be more accessible!

Imagine you've made an indie show on Krita or Flipaclip, or a funny YouTube video to show off your (questionable) comedic humour, or your film that'll surely be the NEXT Napoleon Dynamite! Of course you awnt to make sure it reaches as much people as possible, and is as visible as possible to as many people of many backgrounds.

So you make some subtitles. You make English subtitles or Dutch or whatever your native language is or the language of the country you live in! That's a start, for sure. But you want more languages, only issue is... You don't know them, at all. And you're not going into Duolingo just for that reason, no.

Hopefully this article helps you understand how to get more languages, and where to possibly get them from.

What options do you have?

Apart from learning languages yourself, there is a surprising amount of (albeit few) options.

1. Paying for a service

Although this costs money, you can mostly guarantee that your subtitles (original and translated) have quality of some kind and are accurate to the spoken audio. Services include Caption+ & Amara.

2. No pain, no gain. Grind it out. Learn them languages.

Or you could just... learn the languages for the countries you wanna reach. There are countless of guides (learning in general) and websites for that specific language! (Tofugu's "Learn Japanese" for Japanese)

But that takes too long and you won't be putting your video out for a while if you want it to come with subtitles, out of the box.

3. Ask some friends for translations

We all have friends (atleast I assume you do), and those friends could come from multiple backgrounds or know some languages themselves! As long as they know how to properly translate English to the language you want that they know (Very important detail).

4. Do it yourself

You can just include your native language and the language of where you created the content (or which country you live in), and then when you get an audience enough to demand other languages, you can consider the 3 other options.

...Okay, now how do you these options?

Now that you have chosen something, you have to do it. This will be mostly basic and won't cover EVERYTHING for each option.

1. Paying for a service

This is mostly recommended if you have the money and the age. Here in Belgium, you have to be 18 to be considered a legal adult, and you can pay for invoices (If you're doing this through Captions+). However, if it does turn out you're under the age of 18, you'll need consent from either a guardian or a parent, and hey, between me and you; dear reader... We know parents aren't gonna consent to something like Captions+, unless they support your dream of being a YouTuber or an indie filmmaker. Guardians, not sure. But parents... You'll need some crazy convincing in order to do that.

However, if you are an adult and do have the money to do it, you can go to websites like Captions+ for professional subtitles and captions on YouTube videos.

2. No pain, no gain. Grind it out. Learn them languages.

This one probably takes the most amount of time, since captions and subtitles usually expect you to atleast be fluent in the language you want to make translated subtitles for. If you aren't... You're bound to get some spelling mistakes, I can't say for sure how long it takes to be fluent in any language, but if you're planning to learn and then subtitle; please make sure you're fluent, at the very least.

3. Ask some friends for some translations

This is actually... pretty straightforward. If you have friends that can fluently speak a language you want translations for (Ex. English -> Dutch), then you can ask them for help for the subtitles! If they've directly worked with you on said film or video, then it's better; because everyone's not doing only one thing, they're also helping out in reaching to a more global audience on the internet. Do make sure that your friends in question knows atleast how to translate (as it can be hard for some people, myself included) whatever language the original content is in, to a different language.

4. Do it yourself

If you're not feeling like translating anything and keeping subtitles or captions in the content's native language, then that's alright! After all, it has to prove itself before getting a global audience of some kind. What you mostly need is a text editor (Your operating system's notepad is good enough) or a graphical interface, like Aegisub. Just have good timing, a good ear and good grammar (and spelling) and you'll be ready to go! Just make sure to watch it back in software like VLC in order to make sure you haven't timed anything wrong or if the subtitles aren't all messy.

All the subtitling is done, now what?

Glad you've managed to make your subtitles. But we've gotta understand what platforms allow for their subtitles. Some are basic, others are advanced and some others don't even have subtitles or closed captions.

The basic websites

YouTube

YouTube is... Not sure I have to explain what YouTube is to you. But in 2008, they added closed captions, supporting formats like .srt. Here below is a table of what YouTube supports.

Format name File extension More info
Subrip .srt Only basic versions of these files are supported. No style info (markup) is recognized. The file must be in plain UTF-8.
SubViewer .svb or .sub Only basic versions of these files are supported. No style info (markup) is recognized. The file must be in plain UTF-8
MPsub (MPlayer subtitle) .mpsub "FORMAT=" parameter is supported.
LRC .lrc No style info (markup) is recognized, but enhanced format is supported.
Videotron Lambda .cap This file type is primarily used for Japanese subtitles.

Source: Google Support

This is the table for basic formats. You have to go to the link in order to see options for advanced subtitles.

Vimeo

Vimeo supports SRT and WebVTT files, however, WebVTT is unfortunately very basic for Vimeo. Vimeo recommends saving the subtitles in the UTF-8 format to ensure that special characters appear.

Newgrounds

Newgrounds supports nothing, actually! It's honestly surprising, considering Newgrounds isn't as niche of a website and has a rich history and is globally well known. But alas, your only option is to burn the subtitles into the video or film itself, and that only supports a singular language.

Conclusion

Subtitles are important, so are captions; but they're the same thing, technically. But what's also important is making sure you have multiple languages, too! That way, you can reach as many audiences as possible and make sure people of hard hearing or full-on deaf can enjoy your content as much as possible.

TV already makes channels have to add on subtitles into their broadcasting. In the UK, small channels need 10% of their broadcasting to have captions! Once the channel is established, that's bumped up to 80% of broadcasts! 10% for audio descriptions, and finally, 5% of sign language.

We are soon reaching into an age of accessibility, maybe, maybe not. But we also need subtitles and captions. They're VERY important important to your content in ensuring not only global audiences, but hard hearing and deaf viewers can read along and understand what you're saying or what any of your characters are saying.
